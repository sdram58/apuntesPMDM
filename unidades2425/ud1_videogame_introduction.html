Introduction

Video Game

The definition of video game is very simple:



A video game is a game that relies on electronic media for its development.

It is entertainment software that, by using the capabilities of electronic devices, allows the user/users to interact with a virtual world with the goal of achieving an objective.

Originally, the purpose of video games was entertainment, a purpose that remains in most current video games, although there are also educational and instructional video games.

The electronic devices used to run video games are known as platforms, and today the most widespread platforms are computers, consoles, mobile phones, and tablets.

Evolution of Video Games

Video games are constantly evolving based on the following factors:

  • Technological advancements.
  • New types of devices.
  • Connectivity.
  • Culture.
  • Consumer tastes and preferences.
  • Time available for playing.
  • …

The first three are the most decisive because these factors directly influence the mechanics that video games can have. For example, the advent of touch devices allowed for the creation of mechanics that did not previously exist.

When it comes to video game consoles, it is easier to study their evolution because they can be classified by generations.

Current Market

It is estimated that the global video game market reached $240 billion in 2023.

China, the USA, and Japan are in the top 3 worldwide in terms of revenue.

In Europe, the top 5 markets are Germany, the UK, France, Spain, and Italy.

Spain ranks second in the world in e-sports revenue, just behind Poland.

The video game market is constantly growing.

The reasons for this growth include:

  • The rise in mobile device usage.
  • The expansion of virtual and augmented reality.
  • The evolution of devices.
  • The evolution of associated technologies (machine learning, artificial intelligence, etc.).

Video Game Development Phases

Concept (Analysis)

As with any application, video games start with an idea and its analysis. This analysis determines the aspects of the game:

  • Genre.
  • Gameplay.
  • Characters.
  • Environment.
  • Music.
  • …

The concept is usually accompanied by a storyboard with all the ideas.

Pre-production (Analysis)

With the concept decided, the next step is to define all the elements that make up the game, such as the development of the story, initial versions of the scripts with objectives, character development, etc.

With this information, artists start creating design proposals to give the final shape.

The sound aspects of the game, including effects, music, voices, etc., are also described.

Simultaneously, the general functioning of the game must be specified.

In the technical part, the programming design is carried out, indicating how the game will be implemented, the programming languages, methodologies, etc.

All the previous information aims to generate the GDD (Game Design Document), which specifies all the planning needed for the game's implementation.

Finally, a very basic prototype of the game can be created in this phase.

Production (Implementation)

During implementation, the first step is to create an initial version of the game, which will be gradually improved during implementation.

The first step is to generate all the assets required based on the requirements indicated in the GDD.

Once all or most of the assets are available, the game must be "assembled."

This is where the game engine is used.

Testing

This phase involves fixing the errors detected during production.

Testing usually comes in two types:

  • Alpha:
    • Performed by people involved in development.
    • Its goal is to fix major bugs and improve features.
  • Beta:
    • Performed by people external to the development team.
    • Its goal is to detect minor issues and enhance the user experience.

Post-production

In this phase, the tasks to be performed are:

  • Marketing: promoting the game.
  • Distribution: creating physical/digital copies and delivering them to stores.
  • Maintenance: continuing to detect errors, add improvements, etc.

Marketing and distribution are usually outsourced to external companies known as publishers.

Maintenance helps extend the life of the game and continue generating revenue: DLCs, new features, etc.

Video Game Engines

A video game engine is software that combines the tools and libraries needed to create video games.

Among the functionalities they offer are:

  • Physics engine.
  • Collision management.
  • Graphics rendering.
  • Sound integration.
  • Creation and management of animations.
  • Artificial intelligence for NPCs.
  • Level design.

Video game engines can be classified in different ways, but the most common are 2D engines and 3D engines.

The most popular video game engines are:

  • Unreal Engine: proprietary, 3D, general-purpose.
  • Unity: proprietary, 2D/3D, general-purpose.
  • Godot: open source, 2D/3D, general-purpose.
  • GameMaker Studio 2: proprietary, 2D/3D, general-purpose.
  • MUGEN: freeware, 2D, fighting.
  • RPG Maker: proprietary, 2D, role-playing.
  • Ren'Py: open source, visual novels.

All of the above engines allow for the development of video games for multiple platforms.
